What's Actually Helped Other Small Businesses

From 2025 research on small business tariff adaptation:

  • Supplier negotiation — Most cited as single most effective lever. Suppliers often don't want to lose your business and will work with you on terms, timing, or cost-sharing. Worth a direct conversation.
  • Early, transparent price adjustments — Customers are more likely to accept increases while tariffs are in the news. Waiting erodes margin and makes later increases harder to justify. Incremental beats sudden.
  • Supply chain mapping — Know exactly which items are affected and by how much before making decisions. Prioritize by impact, not by anxiety.
  • Diversification over time — Not overnight overhaul, but gradually adding alternative suppliers in non-targeted countries. Reduces future exposure.

Small businesses absorb more tariff costs than large ones (~54% passed through vs. ~65% for large firms). The disadvantage is real — but so is the opportunity to move faster than big competitors.

If curious, one small step...

Next time you're in touch with your manufacturer, ask one question: "Are there any cost-sharing options or timing adjustments that could help us manage tariff impact together?" Suppliers often have more flexibility than you'd expect.

Operations

Where automation or support could reduce friction without losing the handmade feel.

Possible High-ROI Areas

These tend to matter for brands at this stage — worth considering if any feel relevant:

  • Email flows: Welcome series, abandoned cart, post-purchase. Set up once, runs passively.
  • Contribution margin per order: Actual profit after COGS, shipping, packaging, fees, returns. Hard to make good decisions without it.
  • Inventory-to-cash visibility: What's selling, what's sitting, how that connects to cash flow.

Automation Opportunities

  • Social scheduling: Batch content creation, schedule posts. ~5 hrs/week recovered for ~$15/month.
  • Order notifications: Automated shipping updates, delivery confirmations. Reduces "where's my order?" inquiries.
  • Review requests: Post-purchase flow asking for reviews at the right moment. Builds social proof passively.
  • Abandoned cart recovery: 5–15% recovery rate is typical. Money left on the table without it.
  • Restock alerts: For repeat customers or waitlist items. Low effort, high intent.
  • FAQ/returns self-service: Clear page with common questions reduces support tickets.
  • Inventory low-stock alerts: Notification when SKUs hit threshold. Prevents stockouts.
  • Pre-order deposit flows: Automated for upcoming releases. Captures demand early.

None of these require hiring. Most are one-time setup with occasional maintenance.

Support Considerations

  • Fulfillment help: When does hand-packaging stop being a brand feature and start being a bottleneck? What's the threshold?
  • Customer service overflow: Contract or part-time for peak periods (launches, holidays). Protect founder energy.
  • Social content batching: Someone to handle posting schedule, not creative. Frees mental load.
  • Order/inventory admin: Data entry, tracking, reorder alerts. Low skill, high time drain.
  • Email management: Inbox triage, template responses, escalation to founders for real decisions.

First hire is usually the task you hate, not the task you love. Creative founders over-index on creative roles.

Research Pathways

Shopify app ecosystem for automation (Klaviyo, Gorgias, Loop Returns). What similar-sized brands actually use vs. what gets recommended. True cost of apps when stacked. When to systematize vs. when ad hoc is fine. Fulfillment thresholds — at what volume does 3PL make sense? Part-time VA options for customer service during launches. Simple dashboards for inventory/cash flow visibility without enterprise software. Beehiiv/Mailchimp/Klaviyo comparison for brand at this stage. How to hire first part-time ops person — where to find, what to pay, how to scope. Zapier/Make automations that actually save time vs. ones that create maintenance burden.

If curious, one small step...

Pick the one task that drains the most energy and spend 20 minutes researching one tool or hire that could handle it. No commitment — just know what exists.

Ownership & Funding

It's fine to ignore this section if you're not currently thinking about outside capital. Bootstrapping is a valid long-term choice, not just a phase before "real" funding.

What Staying Independent Preserves

  • Full creative control
  • No dilution
  • No board, no reporting
  • Can stay weird
  • Can move slow if needed
  • Exit on your terms (or never)

What Staying Independent Requires

  • Revenue funds growth
  • Slower scaling
  • Cash flow discipline
  • Wearing all hats longer
  • Saying no to opportunities
  • Personal financial exposure

Comparable: Mr Jones Watches

London-based, 15+ years operating, artist collaboration model. Key differences worth studying:

  • In-house dial printing and assembly — They don't just design, they manufacture. What does that enable?
  • ~150 watches released, ~30 artist collaborations — What's the sustainable cadence?
  • Physical retail presence (Covent Garden store) — Does DTC-only leave something on the table?
  • No custom one-off runs — Clear policy. Does Mad Hudson need one?
  • High volume of inbound design submissions — Artists come to them. How?

Tariffs & Manufacturing (2025)

  • De minimis elimination: No more duty-free under $800 for China/HK. Now 90% tariff or $75 flat fee on low-value packages.
  • DIY Watch Club case study: 80% US revenue, pivoted to Europe after tariffs. Market share there went 6% → 30%. US revenue impact: 20–30%.
  • Industry response: Supply chain diversification (India emerging), batch ordering, tariff engineering (component sourcing), price increases.
  • Hong Kong Watch Fair: Still valuable for meeting manufacturers, understanding component landscape.

Email & Retention

Email generates $36 revenue per $1 spent — highest ROI channel. Core flows for DTC:

  • Welcome series: Can go from $0.50 to $8 per recipient with proper setup.
  • Abandoned cart: 5–15% recovery typical. One SMS in flow can accelerate.
  • Post-purchase: Reviews, referrals, repeat purchase timing.
  • Browse abandonment: Often missing from small brand setups.

Target: ~30% email-attributed revenue, split evenly between flows and campaigns.
