Gs Watch Complete Deck — Multi-Dimensional Analysis

Evaluating against design philosophy, usability principles, and brand coherence.

Dimension 1: Philosophy Alignment

Testing against the four core principles from the Design Philosophy document.

Principle Checklist

  • Frameworks, not conclusions — Lenses section exists, but watches still deliver conclusions about what they "do"
  • Prompts, not prescriptions — Invitation prompts added, but vignettes still prescribe use cases
  • Space, not text — Reflection blanks are present; good use of dashed boxes
  • Paths, not sequences — Document is still strictly linear; no alternative entry points

Issue

Vignettes are prescriptions in disguise

"Ways this has been used" sounds invitational, but the vignettes are still us telling readers what the watch is for. They narrow rather than open.

Example: "I set it before difficult phone calls" implies the Focus Bezel is for phone calls. Someone who'd never use it that way might feel excluded.

Current

"I set it before difficult phone calls. The boundary helps me stay present."

More open

"Some mark beginnings. Some mark endings. Some mark the middle of something they don't want to lose."

Issue

Watch descriptions still declare function

"A rotating bezel creates a colored arc. The minute hand travels through it. When the hand exits the color, something ends."

This is better than before, but still positions us as the authority on what the watch does. The reader is told, not invited to discover.

Opportunity

Reframe watch cards as "observations" not "explanations"

Instead of explaining what the watch does, describe what's physically there and let readers draw conclusions.

Try: "There's a colored sector on the bezel. The minute hand moves. The sector can be rotated. What would you do with this?"

Dimension 2: Structure & Navigation

"Paths, not sequences" — the document should allow multiple entry points.

Issue

Strictly linear structure

The current structure forces: Philosophy → Lenses → Focus Bezel → Day Map → etc. A reader who wants to start with straps or hybrids has no map.

Opportunity

Add a visual navigation map

A single-page overview showing all concepts as nodes. Readers can start anywhere. Each section becomes self-contained.

Opportunity

Make each watch card standalone

Currently, watch cards assume you've read the philosophy section. Each card should work if encountered alone. Add a one-line "lens" reference at the top of each card.

Dimension 3: Visual Design

Assessing warmth, imperfection, space, and coherence.

  • Warm palette — Girard-inspired colors are well-applied
  • Soft edges — Rounded corners throughout
  • Imperfection as intention — Icons are geometric, not hand-drawn
  • Space to breathe — Generous margins, reflection blanks
  • Warm shadows — Some pure black in SVG strokes

Observation

Watch SVGs are polished, not warm

The watch mockups are clean vectors. This works for manufacturing specs, but for a philosophy-driven deck, a slightly hand-drawn quality might reinforce the "folk art DNA" mentioned in the design system.

Consider: Adding subtle stroke variation, imperfect circles, or hand-drawn hour markers to at least one variant.

Opportunity

Lens icons could be hand-drawn

The six lens icons (◯ ▱ → ◎ ≈ □) are Unicode characters. Custom hand-drawn versions would reinforce warmth and differentiate from corporate design systems.

Issue

Strap mockups are too technical

The detailed strap SVGs show construction details (spring bars, buckle pins). This is useful for manufacturing but feels clinical for an invitational deck. Consider softer, more illustrative versions for the philosophy deck, with technical versions reserved for spec documents.

Dimension 4: Language & Tone

Testing for prescriptive vs. invitational language.

Language Audit

Observation

Tone varies by section

The philosophy sections are invitational. The hybrid appendix is prescriptive (appropriately — it's internal spec). The watch cards sit awkwardly in between. Consider making the split explicit: philosophy deck vs. spec deck.

Dimension 5: Conceptual Depth

Are the ideas fully developed? Are there gaps?

Gap

Lenses aren't connected to watches

The six lenses (Position, Container, Momentum, Anchor, Rhythm, Boundary) are introduced, then each watch gets a lens tag, but there's no exploration of how the same watch might serve different lenses for different people.

Opportunity

Add a "lens matrix"

A grid showing each watch against each lens, with question prompts at intersections. This makes explicit that the same object can be multiple tools.

Position Container Boundary Rhythm
Focus Bezel Where is the hand? How much color remains? Where does the arc end? How many sessions today?
Day Map Which zone am I in? How much day is left? When does this zone end? Does this day match yesterday?
Tide Dial High or low? — When does the tide turn? Same rhythm as the sea

Gap

No "anti-personas" or edge cases

The deck implicitly assumes readers will connect with the philosophy. What about someone who finds the whole framing pretentious? Or someone who just wants a watch? Acknowledging these perspectives would make the deck more honest.

Opportunity

Add a "This might not be for you if..." section

Honest acknowledgment of who won't connect with this. Paradoxically, this increases trust with the intended audience.

Dimension 6: Reader Agency

Does the document give readers real choices? Can they meaningfully respond?

  • Reflection blanks exist — Space for writing is present
  • Blanks are functional — In HTML, the blanks are just styled divs, not inputs
  • Questions are open — Some questions have implicit "right answers"
  • Reader can disagree — No mechanism to express "this isn't for me"

Issue

Reflection blanks are decorative

The dashed boxes say "(your words here)" but can't actually receive input. For a printed or PDF version this is fine, but for HTML it feels incomplete.

Opportunity

Make reflection blanks functional

Add contenteditable or textarea inputs. Store responses in localStorage. This transforms the deck from document to tool.

Opportunity

Add a "your lens" discovery flow

A short series of questions that surface which lens(es) resonate most. Not a quiz with a "result" — just a framework for self-reflection. Output could be: "You seem drawn to Boundary and Rhythm. The Focus Bezel and Quarter Chime might be worth exploring."

Dimension 7: Practical Completeness

Does the deck contain what's needed for its purpose?

For Internal Strategy

  • Concept definitions — Clear
  • Visual mockups — Present for all concepts
  • Hybrid guardrails — Explicit
  • Manufacturing considerations — Mentioned but not detailed
  • Timeline / phasing — Not present
  • Cost estimates — Not present

For Partner / Customer Presentation

  • Emotional hook — Philosophy section works
  • Product clarity — Concepts clear, specs light
  • Pricing — Not present
  • Call to action — No next step for reader
  • Contact / waitlist — Not present

Observation

Deck serves two masters

It's trying to be both internal strategy document and external-facing philosophy piece. These audiences need different things. Consider splitting into two versions.

Recommendations

Priority Actions

High Priority

  1. Rewrite vignettes as open observations — Remove specific use cases; describe patterns without prescribing
  2. Add lens tags to every watch card — Make the framework visible throughout
  3. Create navigation map — Allow non-linear exploration
  4. Split into two documents — Internal spec vs. external philosophy

Medium Priority

  1. Make reflection blanks functional — contenteditable + localStorage
  2. Add lens matrix — Show how each watch serves multiple lenses
  3. Hand-draw lens icons — Reinforce warmth and folk art DNA
  4. Add "This might not be for you" section — Honest acknowledgment

Lower Priority (Polish)

  1. Create hand-drawn watch variant for philosophy deck
  2. Soften strap mockups for non-spec contexts
  3. Add discovery flow for lens identification
  4. Include timeline and cost estimates for internal version

Appendix: Specific Rewrites

Focus Bezel — Rewritten for Agency

Current

"A rotating bezel creates a colored arc. The minute hand travels through it. When the hand exits the color, something ends."

"What that 'something' is — a work session, a conversation, a pause — is yours to decide."

Rewritten

"There's a bezel that rotates. There's a colored sector. There's a minute hand that moves."

"What happens when the hand enters the color? What happens when it exits? What would you use this for?"

Day Map — Rewritten for Agency

Current

"The dial is divided into colored zones. The hour hand points to where you are in the day's cycle."

"What each zone means — rest, focus, transition, care — is for you to discover."

Rewritten

"The dial has colors. They shift from cool to warm and back. The hour hand moves through them."

"Some people name the zones. Some don't. What do you see?"

Vignettes — Rewritten as Open Observations

Current

"I set it before difficult phone calls. The boundary helps me stay present."

"25 minutes of writing. When the hand exits, I stop mid-sentence."

Rewritten

"Some mark beginnings. Some mark endings. Some mark middles they don't want to lose."

"Some rotate before. Some rotate during. Some never rotate at all."
