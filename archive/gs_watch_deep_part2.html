WCAG Cognitive Accessibility Checklist

  • Readable fonts — DM Sans (body) and Fraunces (display) are clean, legible choices
  • Sufficient contrast — Text/background ratios meet AA standards
  • No autoplay media — No distracting animations or sounds
  • No flashing content — Static design, no seizure triggers
  • Consistent navigation — Linear scroll works but no jump-to navigation
  • Clear structure — Good headings but some sections are text-heavy
  • Sufficient spacing — Good line height (1.7) but some cards are dense
  • Reading level — Some philosophical sections exceed lower secondary level
  • Predictable layout — Different card formats create cognitive switching
  • Time-free interaction — N/A for static doc, but reflection blanks have no save state

Specific Issues Found

Readability Issue

Philosophy section reading level too high

Flesch-Kincaid analysis of the opening philosophy section estimates Grade 11-12 reading level. Target should be Grade 8 or below for cognitive accessibility.

Example: "This project isn't about our conclusions regarding time. It's about building tools that help you notice your own relationship with it." — This sentence has nested clauses and abstract concepts.

Simplified: "These watches help you see time differently. Not our way. Your way."

Cognitive Load Issue

Six lenses presented simultaneously

The "Lenses for Noticing" section presents six abstract concepts (Position, Container, Momentum, Anchor, Rhythm, Boundary) in a grid with no progressive disclosure. This creates high cognitive load, especially for users with ADHD or processing differences.

Recommendation: Present 2-3 lenses at a time, or offer a "Which lens speaks to you?" flow that reveals one at a time.

Navigation Issue

No table of contents or section anchors

For a document this length (~1700 lines), users with attention or memory challenges need clear wayfinding. Currently, the only navigation is scrolling.

Recommendation: Add a sticky TOC sidebar or top navigation bar with section links.

Format Inconsistency

Card formats vary unpredictably

Some watch cards have "Ways this has been used" sections, some don't. Some have invitation prompts, some don't. This unpredictability increases cognitive switching cost.

Recommendation: Standardize all watch cards to identical format, even if some fields are optional.

Strengths

Strength

Sensory-friendly palette

The warm, muted Girard-inspired colors avoid high-saturation hues that can be overwhelming for sensory-sensitive users. Good choice of cream backgrounds over pure white.

Strength

Generous whitespace

The deck uses adequate margins and padding, giving content room to breathe. This reduces visual overwhelm.

Strength

Reflection blanks signal agency

The dashed-box "your words here" pattern is a clear visual cue that something is expected from the reader. This predictability helps users with executive function challenges.

Part 2: New Watch Concepts

Part 2: Ten New Watch Concepts

Informed by research on: Slow watches (24-hour single-hand), Eone Bradley (tactile ball bearings), BOTTA 24-hour (sun-synchronous), wearable haptics, and cognitive accessibility principles.

Position · Rhythm

Horizon

A 24-hour watch where a single marker (the "sun") travels above and below a horizon line. Above = day. Below = night. No numbers needed.

Innovation: Combines BOTTA's sun-synchronous concept with radical simplicity. Position relative to horizon is instantly readable without learning a new system.

Anchor · Rhythm

Pulse

An almost-blank dial. Tap the crystal and feel the time through haptic pulses. Long pulse = 5 hours. Short pulse = 1 hour. Completely private.

Innovation: Draws from Apple Watch's Taptic Time but makes it primary, not secondary. The visual dial is intentionally empty — time is felt, not seen.

Position · Momentum

Orbit

Three ball bearings on concentric tracks: hours (outer), minutes (middle), seconds (inner). Inspired by Eone Bradley but with three bodies instead of two.

Innovation: Multiple orbiting elements create a "solar system" metaphor. Can be read visually or tactilely. The seconds ball provides meditative movement.

Container · Boundary

Weight

A balance beam that tilts throughout the day. Morning: tilted left. Evening: tilted right. Noon: level. Feel the "weight" of the day shifting.

Innovation: Physicality metaphor — time has mass, the day has weight. Highly intuitive for kinesthetic learners. No numbers needed.

Container · Boundary

Fill

A vessel that fills from bottom to top throughout your waking hours. Wake time and sleep time are user-set. Shows "how much day is left" as a liquid level.

Innovation: Reframes time as a finite resource filling/depleting. Highly intuitive for people with time blindness — "the tank is 60% full."

Position · Anchor

Spoke

Twelve fixed spokes. One spoke lights up (or is textured differently) to indicate the current hour. No moving parts — just activation.

Innovation: Eliminates the need to track moving hands. Position is absolute, not relative. Could work as LED, mechanical reveal, or tactile texture change.

Position · Rhythm

Shadow

A sundial on your wrist. A fixed gnomon casts a "shadow" (projected by internal light or mechanical cam) that moves with the sun's path.

Innovation: Reconnects to oldest timekeeping method. The "shadow" could be actual (outdoor use) or simulated (internal LED + frosted crystal). Deeply intuitive.

Boundary · Position

Threshold

Three horizontal lines represent transitions. A marker moves right throughout the day AND drops down to the next line at each threshold. Position = time, row = phase.

Innovation: Combines linear progress (left-right) with categorical phase (row). Makes day phases explicit while showing within-phase progress.

Anchor · Rhythm

Stone

An organic, pebble-shaped case with no visible dial. A single raised bump (the "marker") slowly migrates around the edge over 24 hours. Time is felt, not seen.

Innovation: Inspired by Eone Bradley's tactile approach but removes all visual time-telling. The object is a worry stone that happens to tell time.

Rhythm · Anchor

Breath

The dial pulses at a fixed breathing rhythm (e.g., 4 seconds expand, 4 seconds contract). Not a timer — a pacer. The time is secondary to the rhythm.

Innovation: Inverts the watch's purpose. Time-telling becomes secondary to nervous system regulation. For anxiety/ADHD users who need grounding more than scheduling.

Innovation Gap Analysis

Gap 1

No one is doing "time as felt sense" for the general market

Eone Bradley serves blind/low-vision users. Apple Taptic Time is buried in settings. No product makes tactile time-telling the primary selling point for mainstream users who want to reduce phone-checking.

Gs Watch position: "Feel time. Don't look at it."

Gap 2

24-hour watches exist but don't serve neurodivergent needs

Slow and BOTTA make 24-hour watches, but they're positioned as "slow life" lifestyle products, not as tools for time blindness or ADHD. The framing is "slow down" (prescriptive) not "see your whole day" (orienting).

Gs Watch position: "Where are you in your day? See the whole thing at once."

Gap 3

No one combines tactile + 24-hour + day zones

Eone is tactile but 12-hour. BOTTA is 24-hour but visual. Day Map has zones but no tactile. No product combines all three.

Gs Watch position: Develop a hybrid concept: 24-hour dial with color zones AND tactile markers at zone boundaries.

Gap 4

Nothing positions watches as "grounding tools" for anxiety

Smartwatches have "breathe" apps, but they're buried in menus. No analog watch is designed primarily as a nervous system regulation tool.

Gs Watch position: The "Breath" concept — a watch that paces, not schedules.

What Would Be Truly Innovative

Innovation Tier 1: Incremental (Expected)

  • Warm color palette on existing dive bezel timer
  • Vegan straps on conventional watch
  • Day zone colors on standard dial

These are table stakes. Many brands could do this.

Innovation Tier 2: Meaningful (Differentiating)

  • 24-hour dial with sun-synchronous zone colors
  • Tactile markers at zone transitions
  • Invitational philosophy (tools for expression, not prescriptions)
  • Haptic hour chimes (vibration, not sound)

These differentiate Gs Watch from Slow, BOTTA, and standard microbrands.

Innovation Tier 3: Breakthrough (Category-Creating)

  • Tactile-primary watch: Dial is intentionally blank or minimal; time is felt through ball bearing position or haptic pulse
  • Breath-pacer watch: Not a timer, but a pacing tool that helps regulate nervous system
  • "Fill" watch: Visual metaphor of container filling/depleting; shows "how much day is left" relative to user-set wake/sleep
  • Companion object separation: Signal lives off the watch (desk object glows when anchor event active); watch stays purely analog

These create a new category. No existing brand occupies this space.

Recommended Focus

Strategic Recommendation

Lead with one Tier 3 concept + two Tier 2 concepts

The temptation is to launch with incremental improvements. But Gs Watch's brand positioning ("time held gently") demands at least one breakthrough concept to prove the philosophy isn't just marketing.

Suggested launch lineup:

  • Focus Bezel (Tier 2) — Warm, accessible, immediate utility
  • Day Map 24 (Tier 2) — 24-hour with sun-synchronous zones
  • Fill or Pulse (Tier 3) — The category-defining piece
