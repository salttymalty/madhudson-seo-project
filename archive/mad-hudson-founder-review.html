02 — Assessment

What's Working

Brand Position

Artist collaboration model is differentiated

Chicago-rooted, story-driven, artist-centric. Not competing on specs or price with microbrands — competing on meaning. Few others are doing this at watch scale.

Market Signal

Early customer response is strong

9 five-star reviews. Customers who engage are enthusiastic. That's not product-market fit yet — but it's early validation that the concept resonates.

B2B Proof

Macy's partnership opens doors

A major retailer saw something worth betting on. It's one data point, but it's a credible one. Can be leveraged for future wholesale conversations.

Operating Model

Lean and controlled

Low overhead, founders close to every decision, no premature scaling. Cash isn't trapped in structure. You can pivot fast if needed.

What Needs Attention

Constraint

Founder bandwidth is the bottleneck

Three people handling everything means nothing scales. Every new initiative competes with existing operations. The first hire question is overdue.

Dependency

Artist model is differentiator AND risk

The thing that makes you special is also a constraint. Artist relationships are personal, hard to systematize, and create concentration risk if a key partner exits.

Gap

Informal systems

No clear customer source data, no documented workflows, no written artist agreements. Works now — becomes liability at scale or during transition.

Exposure

IP and liability undocumented

Trademark status unclear. Artist IP agreements informal or missing. Product liability exposure unknown. Solvable — but needs attention before next inflection.

03 — Questions

Conversations to Have

Not a checklist. A conversation guide. Work through these separately, then compare notes.

B2B Strategy

+

Not "should we do B2B" but "what role does it play?"

  • 01 Is Macy's a proof point you want to replicate, or a one-off you fell into?
  • 02 If a retailer wanted 500 units tomorrow, could you fulfill it? Would you want to?
  • 03 Does "Mad Hudson Design" strengthen the watch brand or dilute it?
  • 04 Is B2B a growth engine or a diversification hedge?

Capacity & First Hire

+

Before you add, understand what you have.

  • 01 How many founder-hours went into the last product launch?
  • 02 What's the longest you could sustain that pace?
  • 03 If you could hire one person tomorrow at $50K, what would they do?
  • 04 What's the task you personally dread or that consistently falls through cracks?
  • 05 How many artist collaborations per year is sustainable?

The first hire is usually the task you hate, not the task you love. Creative founders over-index on creative roles.

Protection & Structure

+

You don't need to restructure now. But know your triggers.

  • 01 Do you have written agreements with each artist covering IP, royalties, and usage rights?
  • 02 Is "Mad Hudson" trademarked? In what classes?
  • 03 If a customer sued over a product defect, what's your exposure?
  • 04 What triggers would make you restructure into separate LLCs?

Artist Pipeline

+

The model's sustainability depends on repeatability.

  • 01 How do you find collaborators? Is there a waitlist?
  • 02 Are you pursuing artists or are they coming to you?
  • 03 What's the vetting process? How do you know if someone's a fit?
  • 04 If your top artist partner left tomorrow, what happens?
04 — Friction Map

Where Things Break

Predictable pressure points for businesses like this. Severity based on frequency and impact.

Manufacturing & Production

MOQ Negotiations

Minimum order quantities force you to bet on demand before you have data. Too few = stockouts. Too many = cash trapped in inventory.

What's current MOQ per SKU? How much capital in inventory?

Sample Approval Cycles

Back-and-forth on color, materials, construction. Each round = weeks. Artists have opinions that don't translate cleanly to manufacturing.

How many sample rounds per watch? Who has final say?

Lead Time Uncertainty

"8 weeks" becomes 12. Launch dates slip. Pre-order customers get antsy. Marketing calendar breaks.

Gap between quoted and actual lead time?

Artist Relationships

Expectation Misalignment

Artists want creative control. Manufacturing wants reproducibility. Founders want timeline. Someone always compromises.

Clear documented roles before kickoff?

Promotional Commitment

Artist agrees to promote. Life happens. Their audience doesn't materialize. You've bet on reach that doesn't arrive.

Promotional obligations in contracts?

Operations

Customer Service During Launches

Pre-order questions, shipping inquiries, returns. Volume spikes. Founders get pulled from strategic work.

Hours spent on CS last launch?

Content Production

Photos, copy, social, email. Every launch needs it. Often last-minute, often bottlenecked on one person.

Who owns content? Is it documented?
05 — Data Gaps

What You Need to Know

Before making strategy decisions, close these information gaps.

Customer Source

Where are customers actually coming from? Instagram? Artist followers? Macy's? Press? Word of mouth? This determines where to invest.

Conversion Path

What's the journey from first touch to purchase? How many touchpoints? What's abandonment rate?

Landed Cost

Full unit cost including manufacturing, artist royalty, packaging, shipping to you. Not just invoice cost.

Contribution Margin

Revenue minus landed cost minus artist share minus fulfillment. What's left per unit to cover overhead?

Founder Time Allocation

Where do the hours actually go? Who does what? What falls through cracks?

Artist Pipeline Health

How many potential collaborators? What's conversion rate? What's lead time from first contact to launch?

06 — Decision Framework

Before Adding Complexity

Before restructuring, hiring, adding product lines, or any major move — run it through these filters.

Solvency
Can we afford this if it fails?
Sanity
Will this reduce stress or add to it?
Signal
Is this responding to real demand?

All three yes: Move.
One shaky: Investigate.
Two no: Wait.

Most businesses don't fail from wrong structure. They fail from wrong timing — moving too fast (overhead before revenue) or too slow (exposure after risk). Use triggers, not timelines.
