Before the watches, a way of seeing.

"I have no desire to express myself. What I wish to do is provide the means of expression for people."

These are some concepts for watches that show time differently. Not a prescribed way. Your way.

They don't tell you what to do. They help you see. Different shapes for different ways of looking.

Two questions to sit with

Time feels like...

I wish time felt like...

The gap between these might show where a tool could help.

Part Two

Core Concepts

Some ideas for pure analog. No screens. No menus.

Boundary · Container

Focus Bezel

A bezel that rotates. A colored arc. A minute hand that moves through it.

When the hand leaves the color, something ends. What that something is — you decide.

What people have noticed

Some mark beginnings. Some mark endings. Some mark middles they don't want to lose.
Some rotate before. Some rotate during. Some never rotate at all.
The color moving behind the hand changes how time feels — contained, not chased.

Position · Rhythm

Day Map

The dial has colors. They shift from cool to warm and back. The hour hand moves through them.

Some people name the zones. Some don't. What do you see?

What people have noticed

"I stopped asking 'what time' and started asking 'what field.' Different question."
"The afternoon zone is when I schedule nothing. The watch reminds me that's on purpose."
"I noticed I always look during the evening shift. That taught me something."

What would you notice about your position?

(your observation)

Momentum · Boundary

Studio Chrono

A meca-quartz chronograph. Push the button — hear and feel the click. The hand starts moving.

The subdial has a colored sector. The hand travels through it. A ritual for beginning.

What people have noticed

The physical click creates a moment. Before click. After click. Different states.
Some use both pushers (start/stop). Some only use start. Never stop.
The colored sector in the subdial is subtle. You have to look for it. That's the point.

(your ritual)

Rhythm · Position

Tide Dial

One hand. Two positions: HIGH and LOW. The hand swings between them over ~12 hours.

For those who think in swells and ebbs. Energy rises. Energy falls. The hand shows where.

What people have noticed

Some sync it to actual tides. Some sync it to their energy. Some sync it to nothing — just watch it move.
The slow arc is almost invisible. You only see change over hours.
HIGH doesn't mean good. LOW doesn't mean bad. Just different.

What rises and falls in your day?

(your tide)

Part Three

Breakthrough Concepts

Stranger ideas. Things that don't exist yet.

Position · Rhythm

Horizon

A 24-hour watch. One marker (the "sun") travels above and below a horizon line. Above = day. Below = night.

No numbers needed. Where is the sun? That's what time it is.

What makes it different Combines BOTTA's sun-synchronous with radical simplicity. Position relative to horizon is instantly readable.

Anchor · Rhythm

Pulse

An almost-blank dial. Tap the crystal — feel time through haptic pulses. Long pulse = 5 hours. Short = 1 hour.

Completely private. No one else knows you're checking time.

What makes it different Apple Watch has Taptic Time buried in settings. This makes it primary. Time is felt, not seen.

Container · Boundary

Fill

A vessel that fills from bottom to top during your waking hours. Shows "how much day is left" as a liquid level.

Wake time and sleep time are user-set. The tank fills relative to your day, not the clock.

What makes it different Reframes time as finite resource. Perfect for time blindness. "The tank is 60% full" is instantly understood.

Position · Momentum

Orbit

Three ball bearings on concentric tracks: hours (outer), minutes (middle), seconds (inner). A solar system on your wrist.

Inspired by Eone Bradley, but with three orbiting bodies. Can be read visually or by touch.

What makes it different Ball bearings create both tactile and meditative dimension. The seconds ball provides calming movement.

Rhythm · Anchor

Breath

The dial pulses at a fixed rhythm. 4 seconds expand. 4 seconds contract. Not a timer — a pacer.

Time-telling is secondary. The rhythm is primary. For grounding, not scheduling.

What makes it different Inverts the watch's purpose. For anxiety/ADHD users who need regulation more than time-checking.

Anchor · Rhythm

Stone

An organic, pebble-shaped case with no visible dial. A single raised bump slowly migrates around the edge over 24 hours.

A worry stone that happens to tell time. Time is felt, not seen.

What makes it different Removes all visual time-telling. The object is for holding, touching, grounding — with time as a side effect.

Part Four

Extended Concepts

More explorations. Some practical, some playful.

Anchor · Boundary

Anchor Ring

Twelve positions around the dial. Each can be "activated" as an anchor — therapy at 9am, pickup at 4pm.

Active anchors glow. You see your day's shape, not its minutes.

What makes it different Moves from "what time is it" to "what's coming." The lit anchors are landmarks, not numbers.

Position · Rhythm

Field

Four quadrants, each with a name: Focus, Connect, Rest, Move. A dot moves through them over 24 hours.

What each field means — you label it yourself. The watch just shows which field you're in.

Why it's different Like Day Map but with user-defined meaning. The names can be changed or left blank.

Rhythm · Anchor

Quarter Tap

Every 15 minutes, a gentle tap on your wrist. Not a notification — a reminder that time passed.

Some find it grounding. Some find it annoying. That's data about you.

Why it's different Turns passive time-checking into active time-awareness. The tap says: "Still here. Still now."

Position · Boundary

Fade

The dial dims as evening approaches. Bright in morning, muted in afternoon, dark after sunset.

Automatic, based on local sunset time. The watch's energy matches yours.

Why it's different Syncs visual energy to circadian rhythm. Reduces "bright screen at night" effect of checking time.

Anchor · Boundary

Companion

Two objects. The watch stays pure analog. The desk object glows when an anchor event is active.

Separation of concerns: the watch tells time, the companion shows context.

What makes it different Keeps the watch pure while adding smart features to a separate object. Best of both worlds.

Container · Boundary

Weight

A balance beam that tilts throughout the day. Morning: left down. Evening: right down. Noon: level.

Feel the "weight" of the day shifting. Time has mass. The day has gravity.

What makes it different Physicality metaphor makes time tangible. Highly intuitive for kinesthetic learners.

Boundary · Position

Threshold

Three horizontal lines. A marker moves right AND drops down at each phase transition.

Position shows time-within-phase. Row shows which phase. Two dimensions of information.

Why it's different Combines linear progress with categorical phase. Makes day segments explicit.

Position · Anchor

Spoke

Twelve fixed spokes. One spoke activates (lights, colors, or textures) to show current hour.

No moving parts. Position is absolute, not relative. The hand doesn't move — the activation does.

Why it's different Eliminates tracking moving hands. Could work as LED, mechanical reveal, or texture change.

Position · Rhythm

Shadow

A sundial on your wrist. A gnomon casts a "shadow" that moves with the sun's path.

The shadow could be actual (outdoor) or simulated (internal LED + frosted crystal).

Why it's different Reconnects to oldest timekeeping method. Deeply intuitive — the shadow IS the time.

Container · Position

Nest

Concentric rings showing nested time scales: hour (center), day (middle), week (outer).

See your position across multiple time horizons at once. Zoom out from the minute.

What makes it different Most watches show one scale. This shows three. Context for the present moment.

Appendix A

Guardrails for Hybrid Concepts

If technology gets added, these are the boundaries.

What would and wouldn't belong

Would Fit

  • Single-purpose additions (one thing well)
  • Opt-in features (default off)
  • Multi-week battery life
  • Graceful degradation (works without app)
  • Setup-only companion app
  • Haptic time-telling
  • Auto-dim based on sunset

Wouldn't Fit

  • Notifications of any kind
  • Fitness or health tracking
  • Multiple screens or menus
  • Daily or more frequent charging
  • Behavior that triggers checking
  • Data collection beyond setup
  • Required account creation

One last question

Which of these would you want on your wrist? Not which is most innovative. Which would you actually wear?

That's the only question that matters.
